1. 
A lot of early-stage B2B founders think:
“If I just get more visibility, users will come.” 

That’s not really the problem.
The real problem is… nobody cares. 

I was reading a discussion where founders shared how they got their first users. 

And the answers were very similar.
I don't mean running ads or writing viral posts 

Not even “launch and they will come” process.

Most of them got their first users through:
→ people they already knew
→ very targeted outreach
→ or by solving the problem manually first 

Which makes sense when you think about it. 

At that stage, nobody is actively looking for your product.

They’re just dealing with a problem; they need a solution. 

Most founders get it wrong by leading with “here’s what I built.”

While the people they’re reaching out to are like, “why should I care in the first place?” 

Especially when:
• they’ve tried other tools before
• they’ve been pitched 100 times
• or they don’t fully trust new products yet 

So your message gets ignored. 

The founders who actually got their first users did something different.

They proved they understood their ICP's problem first. 

So instead of “here’s what I built,”
It could be “I noticed you’re dealing with this… is that still a priority?” 

You may think it's small or stressful, but it actually works. 

If you’re trying to get your first users and it’s not working,
just maybe you haven’t made people feel: “this person gets exactly what I’m dealing with.” 

That's just it, and you need to make a change (very important). 


2. 
Most B2B SaaS founders ignore the easiest place to get their first users. 
(Which is starting with the people they already know) 

I saw a discussion on X (twitter) where founders shared how they got their first users…
and a surprising number of them said (almost) the same thing. 

Their first customers didn’t come from cold outreach, from content, or from ads. 

They came from ex-colleagues, friends, and people in their network.

Which sounds obvious… but most founders ignore it.
Because it feels uncomfortable. 

They think:
“I don’t want to bother people” or “I don’t want to sound like I’m selling” 

So they skip it and go straight to cold outreach instead. 

But here’s the reality, your network is the only place where:
• trust already exists
• people are willing to reply
• someone might actually give you a chance early on 

Not because your product is perfect, but because they already know you. 

The founders who got their first users didn’t overcomplicate it. 

They just reached out and said:
“hey, I’m working on this… I think it could help you with X. Can I show you?” 

Very simple. No funnel, no sequence, no automation.
Just a conversation. 

And that’s often all it takes to get your first few users.
